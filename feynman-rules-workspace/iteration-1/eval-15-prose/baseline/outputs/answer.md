TCP uses a three-way handshake because both endpoints need to agree on the initial state of the connection before any application data can safely flow between them. TCP is a reliable, ordered, byte-stream protocol layered on top of an unreliable network, which means that every byte sent must be acknowledged and every byte received must be placed in the correct position within the stream. To make that possible, each side must learn the other's starting sequence number, confirm that its own starting sequence number was received correctly, and verify that the other party is actually willing and able to communicate. A simpler one-message or two-message exchange cannot accomplish all of these things at once, especially in the presence of packet loss, duplication, reordering, and delayed datagrams that may have been wandering around the network from a previous incarnation of a connection.

The first message, the SYN, is the initiator's announcement that it wants to open a connection and its declaration of the sequence number it intends to use as the starting point for numbering the bytes it will send. The receiver cannot simply assume sequence numbers begin at zero, because doing so would make it trivially easy for stale packets from an old connection, or for malicious injected packets, to be mistaken for legitimate data. By choosing an initial sequence number that varies from connection to connection, each side protects itself against confusion with previous sessions and against certain classes of spoofing attacks. So the SYN is not just a request to talk; it is also a commitment to a specific numbering convention for the rest of the conversation.

The second message, the SYN-ACK, accomplishes two jobs in a single packet, which is why the handshake feels almost like two handshakes folded together. By acknowledging the initiator's sequence number, the responder proves that it actually received the SYN and understood it. By including its own SYN with its own initial sequence number, it makes the symmetric request in the opposite direction, because TCP connections are inherently bidirectional and each direction needs its own independently tracked sequence space. Without this combined message, the responder would have to send two separate packets to set up its half of the connection, which would be wasteful and would also leave a window in which the state on the two sides was inconsistent.

The third message, the final ACK from the initiator, closes the loop by confirming that the responder's SYN was received. Only after this acknowledgement does the responder know that the initiator is still there, that the path back to it works, and that both sides now share a common understanding of where the byte stream begins in each direction. If the handshake stopped after only two messages, the responder would have allocated resources for a connection without ever knowing whether its own SYN reached its peer, which would expose it to wasted state on half-open connections caused by lost packets or by attackers sending forged SYNs from addresses they cannot actually receive replies at.

There is also a deeper reason rooted in the famous problem of agreeing over an unreliable channel. Two parties can never achieve perfect mutual certainty about each other's state through any finite number of messages, but three messages are the minimum that gives each side reasonable confidence that the other has received its most recent commitment. The first message tells the responder the initiator exists and wants to talk. The second tells the initiator that the responder heard it and is willing to talk back. The third tells the responder that its willingness was heard. Anything fewer leaves at least one party guessing, and anything more would be redundant for the purpose of merely opening the connection. The three-way handshake is therefore a carefully balanced compromise between reliability, efficiency, and security, fitting exactly the minimum number of round-trip exchanges needed to bootstrap a reliable bidirectional byte stream over an unreliable network.
