TCP uses a three-way handshake because both endpoints need to agree on the state of a connection before any application data flows, and that agreement has to be mutual rather than one-sided. TCP is a reliable, connection-oriented protocol layered on top of an unreliable network, which means each side must be confident not only that its own send and receive machinery is ready, but also that the other side has confirmed the same. A single message in one direction is not enough, because the sender would have no way to know whether the receiver actually got it. Two messages are still not enough, because while the initiator would learn that the responder is alive and listening, the responder would not yet have proof that its own reply made it back. Three messages are the minimum needed for both parties to verify, in turn, that the channel works in both directions.

The handshake also exists to synchronize the sequence numbers that TCP relies on for everything it does afterward. Every byte sent over a TCP connection is tagged with a sequence number, and the receiver uses those numbers to reassemble data in order, detect losses, discard duplicates, and acknowledge progress. Because each side picks its own initial sequence number independently, and because that number should be unpredictable for security reasons, the two endpoints must exchange and acknowledge each other's starting values before they can interpret any subsequent segment correctly. The first message carries the initiator's chosen starting number, the second carries the responder's chosen starting number along with an acknowledgment of the first, and the third acknowledges the responder's number. Only after all three have been exchanged do both sides share a common frame of reference for counting bytes.

There is also a defensive reason for the third message. Networks can delay packets, duplicate them, or deliver them out of order, and an old connection request that wandered through the network for a long time could otherwise arrive at a host and trick it into opening a phantom connection that the supposed initiator knows nothing about. By requiring the original initiator to send a final acknowledgment that references the responder's freshly chosen sequence number, TCP ensures that stale or spoofed opening segments cannot establish a half-open connection on their own. The responder will not commit to the connection until it sees a reply that could only have come from a party who actually saw its most recent response, which closes the door on a whole class of confusion that a two-message exchange would leave open.

Finally, the three-way handshake is the natural place to negotiate parameters that govern the rest of the conversation, such as the maximum segment size, window scaling, selective acknowledgment support, and timestamps. These options are carried in the same segments that exchange sequence numbers, so by the time the third message has been processed both sides not only trust the channel and agree on numbering, but also share a consistent view of how the connection will behave. In short, three messages are the smallest number that allows mutual confirmation of liveness, mutual synchronization of sequence numbers, protection against stale or forged opening segments, and a clean opportunity to settle on shared options, which is why TCP settled on exactly that shape rather than something shorter or longer.
