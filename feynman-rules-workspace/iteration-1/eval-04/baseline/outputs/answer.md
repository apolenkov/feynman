# Organizational Hierarchy of a Typical Software Engineering Team

A typical software engineering organization is structured as a layered hierarchy, where each layer narrows in scope but increases in strategic responsibility as you move upward. The layers below describe the most common shape used in mid-to-large technology companies.

## 1. Vice President of Engineering (VP)

The VP of Engineering sits at the top of the engineering organization and is usually a member of the executive leadership team, reporting to the CTO or CEO. The VP owns the overall engineering strategy, organizational health, hiring philosophy, budget, and cross-functional alignment with Product, Design, Sales, and Finance. Rather than writing code or running individual projects, the VP focuses on org design, multi-quarter planning, executive communication, and ensuring that engineering investments map cleanly to company-level business outcomes. A VP typically oversees several Directors, each owning a major product domain or platform area.

## 2. Directors of Engineering

Directors sit one level below the VP and are responsible for entire product lines, platforms, or large functional domains (for example: Payments, Infrastructure, Growth, Mobile). They translate the VP's strategy into department-level execution plans, manage a group of Engineering Managers, and own the headcount, hiring, performance calibration, and roadmap for their area. Directors spend most of their time on cross-team coordination, dependency management, technical strategy at the domain level, and developing the managers who report to them. They are accountable for delivery across multiple teams but typically no longer manage individual contributors directly.

## 3. Engineering Managers

Engineering Managers run individual teams, usually composed of 5 to 10 ICs. They are the primary interface between leadership and the people doing the day-to-day building. EMs own team-level execution: sprint planning, technical roadmap, removing blockers, performance reviews, career growth conversations, hiring for their team, and partnership with the team's Product Manager and Designer. A strong EM balances people leadership (1:1s, coaching, feedback, conflict resolution) with technical leadership (reviewing designs, ensuring quality, prioritizing tech debt). EMs report up to a Director and are accountable for both the health of their team and the delivery of their team's commitments.

## 4. Individual Contributors (ICs)

Individual Contributors are the engineers who design, build, test, and operate software. The IC track itself has multiple levels — commonly Junior, Mid, Senior, Staff, Principal, and Distinguished — with seniority determined by scope of impact, technical depth, and ability to lead through influence rather than authority. Junior and Mid engineers focus on executing well-scoped tasks; Senior engineers own features end-to-end; Staff and Principal engineers operate across teams and set technical direction at a level comparable to Directors and VPs respectively. ICs report into Engineering Managers and are the layer where actual product code, infrastructure, and customer-facing functionality is produced.

## How the Layers Work Together

Information and accountability flow in both directions through this hierarchy. Strategy and priorities cascade downward — the VP sets direction, Directors translate it into domain plans, EMs convert plans into team execution, and ICs deliver the work. In the opposite direction, status, risks, technical realities, and customer signals flow upward, enabling leadership to adjust strategy based on ground truth. The healthiest organizations keep this loop tight: short reporting chains, clear ownership at each layer, and senior ICs who can influence strategy directly without needing to climb the management ladder.
